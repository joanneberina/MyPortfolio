Overview

DocuMentor is an electronic visit verification (EVV) solution used to verify visit activity for home health, home care and hospice.

Electronic Visit Verification is a system for tracking work performed, hours, notes, accurate location information and much more about the patient-caregiver interaction, including information about the service provider. EVV applies to care and health services rendered in the home. The purpose of EVV is to ensure that services are delivered to people needing those services and that providers only bill for services rendered.

Target Users

Caregivers

Individuals who provide care and services to clients and carry out overall needs and plan of care. Caregivers can be family members or professional caregivers. Some services are also provided by Therapy Professionals, Behavioral Consultants, Music Therapists, Speech Therapists etc.

Provider Admins and Supervisors

They can be owners of an agency or the provider's employees who manages caregivers, assigns clients and/or set schedules for the caregivers. They make sure that hours used per client are within budget. They also review notes for each service and approves them for billing.

User Experience Audit

Both the CEO and the development team suggested that a UI redesign is needed. When I was evaluating the current state of the app, it was apparent that the dashboard for both the caregiver and admin didn't have a clear visual hierarchy. For example, there were icons that did not have the affordance that it is clickable. It also did not have a text label which makes it unclear what it is for.

This app also has quite a lot of form handling. And if you know about forms, they're not really fun to look at. The format can be a bit overwhelming and again, there a lack of clear visual hierarchy so it falls short of giving the user some guidance on where to start.

Most of the app's input forms are also in a modal dialogue. Although modal dialogs are very effective in attracting a user's attention to focus on the task at hand, one downside is that the window goes away whenever the outside of the dialogue box is clicked and can be disruptive because the user would have to open the modal again to finish the task. A link for viewing and filling out notes which is a major caregiver task and contains a lot of input fields is also in a modal which is not ideal.

User Research Plan and Goals

I chose to do a qualitative research method by conducting remote interviews and usability testing with our customers who are already using our product. I wanted to gain insight on the nuances of how they use the product, understand their motivation and most of all understand why caregivers are not using the app more regularly. I outlined the general user interview goals and specific user interview goals for each target user.

User Interview Goals

For Providers/Admin:

  • Learn how providers and admin utilize the dashboard. Find out what is useful and what is not.
  • Find out about how admins use the app to help in report generation for billing and reimbursement and specifically any challenges they may have in monitoring caregivers.
  • Learn about the scheduling process.

For Caregivers:

  • Learn about how and where they access and use the app.
  • Find out about any challenges in recording their hours, how and when they clock-in and clock-out.
  • Learn about how they utilize the notes section and what their level of engagement is.

For the Business Development Team:

  • Understand how each customer operates and what makes a customer sign-up for the product.
  • Gather feedback on how customers find value in the product.
  • Learn about customer onboarding process and their challenges when onboarding a customer.

Research Data Analysis

After doing remote interviews with 5 caregivers, 5 admins and 2 of our Business Development Managers, I put together my research data by documenting transcripts from the recorded interviews and then did an affinity mapping. I organized the responses based on painpoints vs. needs and separated them according to user types. I then proceeded to further sort them by what feature or functionality the problem is about. By classifying information in this way, it was easier to identify problems and common issues. It was also easier to present it to stakeholders as it is more digestible.

Notes and Hours, Reports and Forms, Budget Monitoring, Usability, Scheduling and Notifications are the areas where problems needed solving.

User and Process Flows

Mapping out the hours and notes submission process helped us identify the different stages that a session go through and how it correlates to the caregiver's hours and notes submission.

Learnings and takeaways

There is no better insight than talking to your customers directly

We sometimes become rooted to what we think our customers are interested in and assume that they will understand our product the way that we designed it. Before joining DocuMentor, features were implemented in isolation without getting customer's feedback and that resulted in poor customer adoption. After conducting interviews, users' feedback revealed that there were a lot of friction points with interactions on the dashboard and forms. And that's the reason why the caregivers are having a hard time using the app or they just don't use it at all. Listening to our customers not only gave me valuable insights but I also learned about how passionate the providers and caregivers are in giving care to their clients which inspired me more to help them solve their problems.

Document your work. Document everything!

Create a document as a single source of truth to ensure a seamless continuation of the product cycle so that if another designer takes over they wonâ€™t have a hard time looking for things. There is also great value in doing a version control of everything that you work on. Make sure that you don't overwrite changes in your design files so you or other people can see the progress and who knows, your explorations may have some value for another problem. I admit that I could have done a better job capturing everything especially ideas from brainstorming sessions. Documenting the discovery process should not be left behind.

Cross-functional collaboration is really helpful.

Since I was the only UX Designer in the team, I relied on our developers for brainstorming and was able to bounce ideas off of them. Designers are not the only ones with great ideas, developers also have good ideas. Consulting with them early on is especially helpful to know how complex a feature or functionality will be. And by doing that, you can easily make adjustments before going full high-fidelity. Our business development team is a trove of valuable information. Because they give product demos, onboard our customers, and also find customers, they receive first-hand information from our customers about their struggles, needs and wants. I really learned a lot from them and had gotten valuable insights.
