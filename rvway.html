The Big RV Picture

Motorhomes, RVs, campervans - there’s an entire industry around these all-purpose vehicles that take you where you want to go and give you a place to stay along the way. They represent the open road, independence, and adventure, and in the U.S. they are wildly popular with all kinds of travelers.

When I started working on the project, I knew very little about the RV industry since RVs are not very popular where I grew up. My first impression was that this was a very niche market. And after doing some market research, the trends and statistics that I’ve gathered from the RV Industry Association (rvia.org) was eye-opening.

People travelling in an RV in the U.S.

25 million people a year

10 million full-time RVers in the US

Typical RV Traveler

Married and 48 yrs. old

Annual income of $68,000

Trends in RV ownership from 2015-2018

2.32% increase in ages 35-44

3.07% increase in ages 25-34

The RV Driver: their needs and painpoints

After getting a clearer understanding of the demographic we learned more their needs and pain points to help us solve this problem: How might we help RV drivers safely get from point A to point B? Understanding why this problem exists is the first step so we asked this question:

How are the needs of a person driving an RV different from someone who drives a regular vehicle?

We learned that people driving an RV worry about things that wouldn’t normally be a concern for someone who drives a regular vehicle. We have also gathered responses on what features our users would like in a navigation app. These are the top responses from our users.

PAINPOINTS

  • Low clearances
  • Bumpy roads
  • Dangerous intersections
  • Gas stations that do/don't have diesel
  • Steep grades
  • Lack of on/off ramp warnings
  • Narrow city streets
  • Not enough information about stops that accomodates RVs

WANTS

  • Points of interest
  • Parking lots
  • Offline usage
  • Gas stations that accomodate RVs
  • Live traffic update
  • Saving routes
  • Campground information
  • Avoiding tolls
  • Clean showers/restroom locator
  • Gas prices
  • Crowd-sourced information

The first solution is not the best solution

Our initial solution was a native mobile app that had all the bells and whistles - it was like Google maps. It had an option to search for a point of interest as a destination, add stops along the route, it featured geo-location and autocomplete. And naturally, it was going to have a turn-by-turn voice navigation.

But this was not the right solution because we later found out that we didn’t have the resources to get the right API which would provide the data for the points of interest. While all the initial features we designed were certainly “nice to haves”, it didn’t really directly impact the problem of safely routing the RV traveler from point A to point B.

Practicing Design Thinking

In a perfect world, providing all of these features and addressing all their pain points would be ideal. But we had to take into account the implementation, timeframe, and resources to come up with an effective solution, and at the same time set a reasonable goal for our product releases. We went back to our research data and really found these insights helpful.

User:
“The fastest route is not always the best route.”

Directions that are provided by other web mapping services is usually the fastest route which is oftentimes, not RV-friendly. The most common painpoint for people travelling in an RV is that they are afraid of getting into an accident. Safety is the number one concern for people traveling in RVs.

User:
“My biggest fear while driving in an RV is getting into an accident.”

Another challenge that we had was implementing a turn by turn voice navigation was not technologically feasible given the time frame that we have. To top it all off, we were not going to build a native mobile app but instead, a desktop web app that is mainly used for planning ahead.

We shifted our focus back to the core functionality of the app which is to navigate the user away from low bridges with their RV’s specifications. So we thought of how we can improve that experience and address the user’s painpoint which is safety.

Turning to qualitative research and validation

We wanted to validate if there was a use-case for a navigation app that is mainly used for planning, so in our succeeding surveys and interviews we included this question:

“When using a navigation app, is turn by turn voice navigation a necessity to your routing experience?”

YES

60%

NO

40%

60% answered YES and 40% answered NO so there really sn’t really a huge gap. But when we looked at qualitative data, RVers were saying that proper planning is key to a safe and successful RV trip and a seasoned RVer will take the time to plan before a big trip. They would decide on what roads and highways to take, where to get gas, stop for the night and so on. Tips like using a checklist document for all essential supplies is also one of the planning tools being utilized in the RV communities on Facebook. Therefore we assumed that there is a use case for it.

Redesigning with the users in mind

The existing app is intended to generate a route that is safe for RVs after users input their vehicle’s specification but the pure unique value which is the navigation was broken. The user interface was also not intuitive. It wasn’t clear what the user is supposed to do first when they get to the map page. Navigating the app can be confusing and overall, the existing solution was just not usable.

We made the user interface more intuitive and easy to use by designing a clean and simple look since our average demographic is older who might not be tech-savvy. We also used a more conversational language and avoided jargon and labels that might be confusing. And to save on production time, the redesigned sidebar requires none to minimal refactoring when converted to mobile.

Having users set up and save their vehicle profile is a crucial part of generating a route that is custom to their RVs. On the add a vehicle form, we fixed how the labels and fields are laid out in a much clearer format.

The directions page has several sections that shows similar information that you would see on Google maps but one important section is the part that shows where the low bridges are and letting the user know that their RV is being routed away from low clearances, dirt roads or steep grades.

Iterations based on usability testing

After conducting usability testing, we did some iteration based on our users feedback.

The hamburger menu

Nowadays it might be a typical element of interaction, but it is still highly debatable and it has its pros and cons. The first symbol that we used was different, it was not the usual bread-meat-bread hamburger. And we found that the users didn’t really know what the symbol meant. It also kind of looked like a logo so they didn’t know to click on it that‘s why we went back to the typical equal horizontal lines.

Vehicle interaction

Instead of listing just the name of the vehicles, that the user would need to click in order for them to see the details, we designed an interaction where the user can expand each of their vehicles and then they can see the details.

Directions page

To help users remember what vehicle that they are routing with, we added some sort of breadcrumb so they can see the vehicle they are using for that route.

Learnings and takeaways

When in doubt about how much the user will understand something or care about a certain feature, get their feedback and validate ideas. We would initialy rely on our assumptions but without validation from users, we might end up with a product that users are not gonna love or worse, not use at all. Users will always have a different perspective of what is useful and what is not.

“Waiting too long to get that feedback is wasteful”

But also getting feedback from teammates, especially the developers is just as important. I think that if we involved our developers more with the ideation process or if we got feedback early on before we did a ton of work, we would have made informed design decisions because we would know if the solution we were designing can even be implemented or if the resources that are required are available.
